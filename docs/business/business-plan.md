# RouteScout Business Plan

## Purpose

This document is the working business plan for RouteScout.

It is intended for founder alignment over the next few days:
- what we are building
- who it is for
- how it can make money
- why it could become strategically valuable

This is not a formal investor memo.
It is a practical founder plan.

## Business Summary

RouteScout is a running route planning product.

It helps runners find routes that fit the kind of run they want before they head out.

The product is strongest for runners who:
- are in unfamiliar places
- want a route that fits a workout goal
- want to explore without wandering
- want to trust the route before they start

RouteScout is not just a route calculator.
It is not mainly a route library either.
It is a route scouting and route selection product that creates a route for the runner based on
their situation, goals, location, and available time.

## Product Vision

RouteScout should become the best tool for:
- planning a run in a new place
- selecting a route that matches real preferences
- collecting and reusing routes and route specs
- turning route planning into a premium product experience
- replacing 20 to 30 minutes of manual map research with a better answer

The long-term product model is:
- web app for planning
- iPhone app for full product use and navigation

## MVP Product Strategy

The fastest credible path is:

### Phase 1

Launch the planning product as soon as possible:
- web planner
- route generation
- plan selection
- saved plans
- route comparison

This gets a usable product into the market quickly.

### Phase 2

Add the iPhone MVP:
- full planning support
- saved route access
- route review before a run
- basic route use / navigation support

This is the phase where the product becomes a real consumer app rather than just a planner demo.

## Why The Product Can Matter

Current alternatives each fail in part:
- generic map apps are not built for running route quality
- workout apps are not strong route planners
- trail apps do not solve urban and travel running well
- run trackers mostly help after the run, not before it

RouteScout can occupy a useful middle ground:
- better than generic maps for runners
- better than generic fitness apps for route selection
- more premium and more intentional than a utility-only planner
- more personalized than relying on routes uploaded by other people

## Initial Target Market

The initial target should be focused, not broad.

Best early segments:
- business travelers
- vacation runners
- serious runners who want precise route fit
- premium-product runners who already use iPhone and Nike- or Apple-adjacent products

Why this target works:
- the pain is real
- the willingness to pay is better than average
- the brand direction fits them
- the use case is easy to explain

## Positioning

RouteScout should be positioned as:
- premium
- useful
- running-specific
- planning-first
- intelligent without feeling gimmicky
- custom-route-first

It should not position itself as:
- a generic map app
- a social running network
- a race-training logbook
- a trail-only app
- a route database whose main value is showing previously shared routes

## Core Differentiation

The strongest product distinction is:
- RouteScout scouts the route for you

The user should be able to say:
- "I am here"
- "I want this kind of run"
- "I have this much time"
- "What could I do?"

And the product should return a route that feels like a thoughtful runner had already researched
the map, tested the tradeoffs, and picked the best option.

Shared routes can still exist in the product, but they should not be the main source of route
value.

## Pricing Direction

The simplest early pricing model is:
- free tier
- paid subscription tier

### Possible Free Tier

- limited route generation
- a small number of saved plans
- basic route cards

### Possible Paid Tier

- unlimited route generation
- unlimited saved plans and saved routes
- premium route comparison
- richer route scouting features
- better collection features
- cross-device continuity

The likely paid range is:
- consumer subscription, roughly in the low monthly app-subscription range

The exact number does not need to be locked immediately.
The founder plan should focus first on:
- whether we believe users will pay
- which features are clearly premium

## Revenue Logic

The early revenue thesis is:
- users who travel or care about training precision will pay for a better route planning tool
- premium runners are used to paying for performance or convenience tools
- a focused route-planning product can monetize better than a generic free utility

Early monetization should be simple.
Do not try to launch with a complex marketplace or ad model.

## Go-To-Market Logic

Early growth should come from:
- founder-led marketing
- product design and visual quality
- social sharing of route cards and route picks
- travel-running and city-running content
- App Store presentation once iPhone MVP exists

Initial channels to test:
- short-form social content
- running communities
- travel and running crossover audiences
- personal outreach and founder storytelling

## Strategic Value

The business is not only about subscriptions.

RouteScout could also become strategically valuable because it sits at the intersection of:
- running
- planning
- mobility
- premium consumer software

That makes it potentially interesting to:
- running app companies
- fitness ecosystem companies
- map or mobility platforms
- brand-driven running companies

## Nike Acquisition Logic

If Nike is viewed as a serious long-term acquisition target, RouteScout becomes more attractive if
it demonstrates:
- premium brand fit
- strong iPhone execution
- route selection as a differentiated product surface
- a collector-like route identity system
- a loyal niche of runners who care about route quality

The product should feel compatible with a Nike ecosystem without depending on Nike.

## Founder Roles

Current intended split:
- you: product, design, engineering, brand, execution, marketing
- co-founder: financing, business support, company support

This is important because the operating reality is not a balanced labor split.
That should affect compensation structure, even if equity is currently equal.

## Core Business Risk

The main risks are:
- building too much before shipping
- unclear willingness to pay
- overestimating how much route quality can be guaranteed with available data
- giving away too much labor for too little guaranteed compensation

The right response is:
- ship the web planner fast
- get the iPhone MVP running quickly after
- test demand early
- keep developer pay explicit

## Three-Month Objective

The next 3 months should aim to prove:
- the product can ship
- the product can attract early users
- the product can convert some users into paying customers
- the product is worth continuing to invest in

That is a much better near-term business target than trying to perfect the whole company story
before launch.

## Near-Term Business Goal

The immediate business goal should be:
- get the planner web app up quickly
- build the iPhone MVP in the following weeks
- establish the first paid customer base
- create a strong premium brand presence

## Recommended Founder Conversation

The founder conversation should focus on:

1. product scope for the next 3 months
2. who is doing what work
3. what budget is being committed
4. what compensation is guaranteed
5. what success milestones unlock bonuses
6. what the company is trying to prove before deciding on the next phase

## Recommendation

The strongest founder proposal is:
- small but real budget
- defined 3-month build phase
- explicit weekly compensation
- milestone bonuses tied to traction
- shared intent to explore acquisition only after product traction exists
