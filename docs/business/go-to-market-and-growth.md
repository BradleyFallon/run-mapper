# RouteScout Go-To-Market And Growth Proposal

## Purpose

This document turns the current launch ideas into a practical founder proposal.

It is written for an internal planning conversation, not as outside-facing marketing copy.

The goal is to answer:
- how RouteScout should launch
- what order the growth steps should happen in
- how pricing and beta should work
- how subscriber milestones should be defined

## Recommendation Summary

The right near-term strategy is:

1. secure the brand surface
2. ship the web planner quickly
3. get feedback from a small invited group
4. refine the product
5. release the iPhone MVP through beta first
6. soft-launch publicly with light promotion
7. push harder only after reliability, reviews, and retention look real

This is a better strategy than trying to market aggressively before the product is stable.

## Phase 0: Brand And Channel Setup

Before product launch work begins publicly:
- secure the main web domain
- reserve the main social handles
- align the name, logo, and visual identity
- create a lightweight landing page or waitlist page if needed

Goal:
- prevent brand fragmentation
- make the product look intentional from the start

## Phase 1: Planner Web App MVP

The first public-facing product should be the planner web app.

Reason:
- it is the fastest path to a usable product
- it is the easiest place to refine planning UX
- it gives a concrete thing to demo and test

Initial release goal:
- a working planner web app
- clear Scout Specs
- route generation
- route comparison
- basic saved plans / saved routes behavior

### Immediate Testing Plan

Start with:
- friends
- trusted runners
- a few travel-heavy users
- a few technically minded runners

What to ask them:
- did the route feel trustworthy?
- was the plan setup clear?
- was the route output useful?
- what was confusing?
- what would make them actually use it?

This should be a feedback loop, not a broad launch.

## Phase 2: Founder-Led Beta Growth

Once the planner is usable:
- post about it selectively
- invite beta users
- collect structured feedback
- improve the product before scaling attention

Good early channels:
- personal network
- running communities
- travel-running crossover communities
- a small number of relevant subreddit posts
- founder-led social posts

Important note:
- Reddit can be useful, but it should not be the center of the plan
- the better early audience is people who already feel the route-planning pain

## Phase 3: iPhone MVP And TestFlight Beta

After the planner is working well enough:
- build the iPhone MVP
- release it through beta first
- treat this as the full-product validation phase

Beta users should be called:
- `beta testers`

The main goal here is not scale.
The goal is:
- route trust
- app reliability
- subscription plumbing
- better understanding of actual user behavior

## Phase 4: Soft Launch

After the iPhone MVP is stable:
- move from beta to production
- launch publicly
- keep promotion light
- focus on quality and early reviews

This phase should prioritize:
- fixing problems fast
- improving route quality
- improving onboarding
- earning genuine positive reviews

Do not aggressively scale marketing yet.

## Phase 5: Early Growth

Only after the following are true should growth efforts expand:
- the app is reliable
- onboarding is clear
- the route output is strong
- hosting is stable
- App Store reviews are building

Then:
- increase founder-led social output
- increase promoted posts carefully
- target narrow high-fit audiences
- test branded content and story-based ads

## Early Target Audience

The best early customer is:
- a runner who runs regularly and travels often

Why this segment matters:
- the pain is real
- they are more likely to pay for planning help
- they care about route quality
- they are easier to target with clear messaging

Secondary audiences:
- local city explorers
- technical runners with specific workout needs
- runners who want novelty close to home

## Pricing Recommendation

Keep pricing simple.

Recommended public launch price:
- `$4.99 / month`

Recommended introductory offer:
- first `3 months` at `$0.99 / month`

This is easier to explain than a more complicated bundle.

## Beta Tester Offer Recommendation

Do not give away a full year.

Recommended beta-tester reward:
- `6 months free`

Reason:
- generous enough to feel meaningful
- does not give away too much value
- keeps the product feeling premium

If a shorter offer is preferred, the fallback is:
- `3 months free`

## Subscriber Milestone Definitions

Milestones should be defined clearly.

### Activated Subscriber

An activated subscriber is a user who:
- starts a subscription trial, paid subscription, or beta offer-code subscription
- completes onboarding
- uses the product meaningfully at least once

This is the right milestone definition for the first bonus.

### Paying Subscriber

A paying subscriber is a user whose subscription has converted to billing.

This should be tracked as a core business KPI even if it is not the milestone trigger.

## Recommended Milestone Proposal

If the founder goal is to tie compensation to concrete traction while still counting trials:

- `$5,000` bonus at `100 activated subscribers`
- `$10,000` bonus at `1,000 activated subscribers`

Reporting should still separate:
- activated subscribers
- paying subscribers
- current trial users
- churned users

That avoids confusion while keeping the milestone structure simple.

## Why This Proposal Is Better

This proposal is better because it:
- matches how early consumer apps actually launch
- avoids spending heavily before the product is solid
- keeps the growth plan realistic
- lets early traction count even if many users start in trial
- keeps the focus on shipping, testing, and refining

## Suggested Founder Proposal Language

Use language like:

"I think the right launch strategy is to get the planner web app live first, test it with a small
group, improve it, then build and release the iPhone MVP through beta before we push hard on
growth. I want us to keep marketing disciplined: light founder-led promotion first, then a soft
launch, then a bigger push only after reliability and reviews are strong. For pricing, I think we
should be cheap and easy to try, like $4.99 per month with a launch intro offer. For milestone
bonuses, I think we should define them around activated subscribers so early traction counts,
instead of pretending only fully paid users matter at the very beginning."
