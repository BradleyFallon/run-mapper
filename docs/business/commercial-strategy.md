# RouteScout Commercial Strategy

## Purpose

This document is the consolidated commercial plan for RouteScout.

It combines the core market view, acquisition strategy, funnel logic, subscription model, and
six-month traction goals into one active reference.

## Product Position

RouteScout is a premium running route-scouting product.

Its core promise is:
- tell RouteScout where you are
- tell it what kind of run you want
- get a route that feels like an experienced runner already researched it for you

This is different from:
- generic map apps
- route databases
- run trackers that mainly help after the run

## Target Market

### Broad Market

The broad opportunity starts with U.S. running participation.

Planning assumption:
- U.S. running/jogging participation is above `50 million`
- U.S. iPhone-runner TAM is roughly `30 million`

This is not the practical target.
It is the outer market.

### Core Demographic

The strongest early customer is:
- a regular iPhone runner who cares enough about route quality to want help planning

Best early segments:
- travelers who want good runs in unfamiliar places
- premium-product runners who value convenience and trust
- technical runners who care about distance, elevation, surface, or interruptions
- city runners who want novelty without chaos

Working planning assumption for the strongest-fit early audience:
- roughly `3 to 6 million` U.S. iPhone runners

That is a planning estimate, not a formal published market count.

## Acquisition Strategy

RouteScout should not depend mainly on paid ads.

The preferred acquisition mix is:
- SEO and search capture
- App Store discovery
- founder-led content
- running communities and grassroots exposure
- referrals and shared Route Cards
- selective paid amplification once organic messages work

### Primary Entry Surface

The main top-of-funnel surface should be:
- the web planner

Why:
- it is the fastest product to ship
- it is the easiest place to communicate the value clearly
- it lets users invest intent before asking for a bigger commitment

### Best Low-Cost Channels

Highest-value early channels:
- search pages for high-intent running queries
- App Store optimization
- founder-led social content
- route-card sharing
- running clubs and local running groups
- stickers, QR handouts, and race/event presence
- travel-running communities
- referrals

Paid promotion should be used as:
- a multiplier on messages that already work

not:
- the foundation of demand

## Funnel Model

### 1. Awareness

Users discover RouteScout through:
- search
- App Store search
- social posts
- shared Route Cards
- referrals
- community or grassroots exposure

### 2. Planning Engagement

The user enters:
- start location
- distance or time
- route preferences
- terrain or surface priorities

This is the key emotional moment.
They are no longer browsing.
They are imagining a real run.

### 3. Account Creation

Recommended gate:
- let the user begin planning without an account
- require account creation to reveal, save, or claim the route

This works better than asking for signup before value is felt.

### 4. Free Real Use

After signup, the user should be able to:
- save the route
- open it on iPhone
- follow it
- track the run in a limited but real way

This is important because RouteScout likely proves its value best in actual use.

### 5. Upgrade Trigger

The best upgrade pressure is:
- more scouting volume
- more planning depth
- more saved value
- more reuse
- more intelligence

not:
- making the app feel unusable until someone pays

## Subscription Strategy

### Free

Free should feel real enough to:
- run with the app
- follow a route
- track runs
- set basic goals
- try limited route scouting

Recommended limits:
- one active Scout Spec
- one active route
- low route-generation cap
- limited saved history

### Plus

Plus should be the main paid product.

It should unlock:
- higher or unlimited route scouting volume
- multiple Scout Specs
- saved route collections
- route comparison
- route explanations
- trip route packs
- better planning reuse

Positioning:
- `Run with RouteScout for free`
- `Plan with RouteScout seriously on Plus`

Recommended price:
- `Plus = $2.99 / month`

### Pro

Pro should be a later intelligence tier, not just a limit-removal tier.

It should unlock:
- learned preferences
- adaptive route suggestions
- personal route memory
- coaching-style route recommendations
- explanation of why a route fits the runner specifically

Positioning:
- `Pro = RouteScout knows you`

Recommended price:
- `Pro = $4.99 / month`

## User Progression

The intended progression is:

1. discover RouteScout
2. try the planner
3. create an account to claim a route
4. use the route on iPhone for real
5. build trust and habit
6. upgrade to Plus for more planning depth
7. later upgrade to Pro if personalization becomes meaningful

This progression matters because:
- habit and trust come before monetization strength
- route generation is likely the more expensive feature surface
- real run use gives valuable product data

## Six-Month Post-Release Goals

These are the recommended targets for the first six months after iPhone release.

### Base-Case Goal

- `800-1,000` activated users
- `120-350` paying customers

### Strong Outcome

- `1,000-2,500` activated users
- `300-800` paying customers

### Stretch Goal

- `1,000+` paying customers

That should be treated as upside, not the base planning assumption.

## Strategic Aim

The near-term business aim is not just subscription revenue.

By month 6, RouteScout should aim to be:
- launched on web and iPhone
- credible as a premium product
- producing real user traction
- showing working billing and retention signals
- ready for serious partnership or acquisition outreach

The company should plan to actively pursue strategic conversations by month 6.
It should not assume a sale is guaranteed by then.

## Bottom Line

RouteScout should grow by:
- offering real value for free
- charging for planning depth and intelligence
- using the web planner as the main top-of-funnel surface
- relying on search, community, sharing, and product quality before heavy paid ads

The business case works if RouteScout becomes:
- a premium route-planning habit for a valuable niche of runners
- with enough traction and brand quality to matter strategically
