# RouteScout U.S. iPhone Runner Market And Acquisition Plan

## Purpose

This document summarizes:
- the U.S. iPhone runner market relevant to RouteScout
- the core demographic RouteScout should target first
- how RouteScout should acquire users
- what subscription goals make sense in the first 6 months after iPhone release

It is a practical planning document, not a formal investor market model.

## Market View

### 1. Broad U.S. Running Market

The broadest relevant starting point is U.S. running and jogging participation.

SFIA reported in February 2025 that:
- running/jogging surpassed `50 million` U.S. participants in 2024

Source:
- SFIA Topline Participation Report summary: https://sfia.org/resources/sfias-topline-participation-report-shows-247-1-million-americans-were-active-in-2024/

That is the broad behavioral market.

### 2. U.S. iPhone Runner TAM

RouteScout is intentionally iPhone-first on the mobile side.

Statcounter’s U.S. mobile OS data showed:
- iOS at `59.77%` mobile OS share in the United States in January 2026

Source:
- Statcounter U.S. mobile OS market share: https://gs.statcounter.com/os-market-share/mobile/united-states-of-america/

If we apply that to the broad runner base:
- `50 million` U.S. runners x `59.77%` iPhone share
- rough iPhone-runner TAM = about `29.9 million`

This is not a precise count of unique iPhone runners.
It is a useful planning estimate.

## Core RouteScout Demographic

RouteScout is not for every runner equally.

The strongest initial customer is:
- an iPhone runner who runs regularly and cares enough about route quality to want help planning

The best-fit early subgroups are:
- frequent travelers who want a run in unfamiliar places
- premium-product runners who use iPhone and are willing to pay for convenience
- technical runners who care about distance, elevation, pavement, or interruptions
- city explorers who want discovery without chaos

## Core Demographic TAM Estimate

The broad iPhone-runner TAM is about `30 million`.

The more realistic core RouteScout demographic is a subset of that.

A reasonable early planning assumption is:
- `10%` to `20%` of U.S. iPhone runners are plausible strong-fit users for RouteScout

That implies:
- low-end core demographic TAM: about `3.0 million`
- high-end core demographic TAM: about `6.0 million`

This is not a sourced population statistic.
It is a planning estimate based on RouteScout’s actual product fit:
- runners who care about planning enough to pay
- runners who feel route-selection pain strongly enough to want help

## Why This Market Is Attractive

RouteScout does not need to win the whole running market.

It only needs a small share of a useful premium niche.

That niche is attractive because:
- the pain is real
- the value proposition is easy to explain
- the product is differentiated
- the iPhone audience is commercially stronger than average

If RouteScout eventually reached even:
- `0.1%` of the broad U.S. iPhone-runner TAM

that would be roughly:
- `30,000` users

If it reached:
- `0.1%` to `0.3%` of the estimated core demographic TAM

that would be roughly:
- `3,000` to `18,000` users

That is enough to matter for a focused subscription business.

## Acquisition Strategy

RouteScout should not rely mostly on paid ads.

The right acquisition mix is:
- search
- App Store discovery
- founder-led content
- community and grassroots exposure
- referrals
- selective paid amplification

## Main Acquisition Channels

### 1. Web Planner As The Main Top-Of-Funnel

The web planner should be the primary acquisition surface.

It is the easiest place to:
- get people curious
- let them scout a route
- let them invest intent
- convert them to account creation
- hand them off to the iPhone app

### 2. SEO / Search Capture

Google’s SEO guidance makes the key point clearly:
- SEO is about helping users find your content and decide to visit it through search

Source:
- Google SEO Starter Guide: https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/seo-starter-guide

For RouteScout, the best search pages are likely:
- best running route in `[city]`
- safe morning run in `[city]`
- flat 3 mile run in `[city]`
- hotel run near `[district or conference center]`
- hill workout route in `[city]`

These should lead into the planner, not just into generic content pages.

### 3. App Store Discovery

Apple’s App Store docs emphasize discoverability through:
- search metadata
- product page quality
- ratings and reviews
- custom product pages
- promoted in-app purchases
- editorial featuring

Sources:
- App Store discoverability: https://developer.apple.com/app-store/discoverability/
- App Store search: https://developer.apple.com/app-store/search/
- App Store get started: https://developer.apple.com/app-store/get-started/

For RouteScout, this means:
- good screenshots
- strong subtitle and keyword strategy
- clean product positioning
- fast review collection once quality is good

### 4. Founder-Led Social Content

Best content themes:
- "I’m in a new city and RouteScout found this run"
- "Safe early-morning hotel run"
- "Race-prep hill route"
- "Explore a city by running it"

This should feel like useful, aspirational proof rather than generic brand posting.

### 5. Community And Grassroots

Low-cost channels:
- running clubs
- local run groups
- race expos
- stickers with QR codes
- meetup/event handouts
- local running stores
- coaches
- conference and travel communities

This is especially strong for RouteScout because:
- the product is easy to demo
- the use case is highly relatable
- targeted in-person exposure can be cheap

### 6. Referral And Sharing

The product should encourage:
- route sharing
- Route Card sharing
- invite-a-friend loops
- "scout this route type" links

This creates a low-cost growth flywheel:
- more users
- more routes planned
- more sharing
- more discovery

## Six-Month Post-Release Goals

These targets are for the first `6 months after the iPhone app release`.

Use two metrics:
- `activated users`
- `paying customers`

### Definitions

Activated user:
- a user who starts a trial, paid subscription, or qualifying beta/launch offer and actually uses
  the product

Paying customer:
- a user whose subscription has converted to billing

## Recommended Goal Bands

### Conservative Case

By month 6 after iPhone release:
- `100-300` activated users
- `30-120` paying customers

### Good / Base-Case Outcome

By month 6 after iPhone release:
- `400-1,000` activated users
- `120-350` paying customers

### Strong Outcome

By month 6 after iPhone release:
- `1,000-2,500` activated users
- `300-800` paying customers

### Stretch Goal

By month 6 after iPhone release:
- `1,000+` paying customers

This is possible, but it should be treated as upside rather than the base planning case.

## Recommended Monthly Goal Ladder

For a good outcome, a reasonable target path after iPhone release would be:

### Month 1

- `50-100` activated users
- `0-20` paying customers

### Month 2

- `125-225` cumulative activated users
- `20-50` cumulative paying customers

### Month 3

- `225-400` cumulative activated users
- `50-100` cumulative paying customers

### Month 4

- `400-600` cumulative activated users
- `80-160` cumulative paying customers

### Month 5

- `600-800` cumulative activated users
- `100-240` cumulative paying customers

### Month 6

- `800-1,000` cumulative activated users
- `120-350` cumulative paying customers

This is the target range I would use for planning.

## Strategic Interpretation

If RouteScout reaches:
- around `1,000` activated users
- and a few hundred paying customers
- with strong branding and good product reviews

that is enough to support:
- founder confidence
- further growth investment
- partnership conversations
- early acquisition outreach

It is not necessary to have a huge user base for RouteScout to become strategically interesting.
It needs a believable premium niche with real engagement.

## Bottom Line

Planning assumptions:
- broad U.S. iPhone-runner TAM: about `30 million`
- strong-fit core RouteScout demographic: about `3 to 6 million`

Acquisition should come from:
- web planner SEO
- App Store discoverability
- founder-led content
- grassroots running-community exposure
- referrals
- selective paid amplification

Six-month post-release target:
- aim for `800-1,000` activated users
- aim for `120-350` paying customers
- treat `1,000 paying customers` as a stretch goal, not the base case
